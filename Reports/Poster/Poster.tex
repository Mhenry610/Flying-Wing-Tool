  \sectionbar{Abstract}
  The Flying Wing Tool is a preliminary-design environment for tailless aircraft built around one shared project model. The research problem addressed here is workflow fragmentation: geometry, aerodynamics, structure, mission, and export are often separated across different tools, which slows iteration and allows assumptions to drift. The implemented environment keeps those tasks tied to one aircraft definition. A representative blended-wing case study plus an independent BARAM CFD path were used to show both the value and the present limits of that workflow.

  \sectionbar{Research Gap and Contribution}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Flying-wing design is tightly coupled: planform and twist changes affect trim, loading, structure, and manufacturability together.
    \item Fragmented tool chains slow iteration because geometry and assumptions are repeatedly re-entered.
    \item The main contribution is software integration: one serialized project state supports geometry, aerodynamics, mission, structure, and export.
  \end{itemize}

  \sectionbar{Implemented Workflow}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item GUI tabs: Geometry, Analysis, Mission, Structure, and Export.
    \item Shared state: one project object stores wing definition, trim assumptions, mission settings, and outputs.
    \item Service layer: geometry/aerodynamics, mission/propulsion, wingbox-oriented structure, and export.
    \item Reproducibility path: \texttt{run\_full\_analysis.py} reloads saved projects for repeatable reruns.
  \end{itemize}

  \sectionbar{Why the Integrated Loop Matters}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The same spanwise sections drive aerodynamic loading, structural sizing, and exported geometry.
    \item Twist optimization is written back into the same project state used for later structural and mission analysis.
    \item One saved case can expose aerodynamic promise together with a governing structural or drag limitation.
  \end{itemize}

  \sectionbar{Case Study Interpretation}
  The case study shows why the shared-state workflow matters. The same saved aircraft produces a coherent cruise solution, a usable twist distribution, a lightweight but not yet fully feasible structure, and a mission-level energy summary. The tool therefore surfaces coupled design consequences rather than isolated outputs.

  \vspace{0.2\baselineskip}
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth,height=2.2in,keepaspectratio]{\reportfig{intendedvalidation2_baram_vs_aerosandbox_ld.png}}
  \tightcaption{Separated \(L/D\) comparison so the coefficient bars are not visually compressed.}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item Independent CFD path: incompressible RANS, standard \(k\)-\(\epsilon\), symmetry half-model, convergence after 604 SIMPLE iterations.
    \item After rescaling to the thesis reference quantities, lift is directionally close: \(C_L=0.273\) in BARAM versus \(0.300\) in the repository cruise analysis.
    \item Drag differs materially: \(C_D=0.0341\) in BARAM versus \(0.0129\) in the repository cruise analysis, reducing \(L/D\) from 23.21 to 8.00.
    \item The CFD drag is pressure dominated: 77.2\% pressure drag and 22.8\% viscous drag.
    \item The centerbody plus junction occupy only 32.2\% of projected planform area but account for 94.3\% of the drag share, indicating a centerbody-dominated pressure-drag mechanism.
  \end{itemize}

  \sectionbar{Mesh-Sensitivity Study}
  \begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}>{\bfseries}l>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}X>{\raggedleft\arraybackslash}p{0.14\linewidth}>{\raggedleft\arraybackslash}p{0.14\linewidth}>{\raggedleft\arraybackslash}p{0.14\linewidth}@{}}
    \toprule
    Condition & Cells (coarse \(\rightarrow\) medium \(\rightarrow\) medium 2 \(\rightarrow\) fine) & \(\Delta C_D\) & \(\Delta C_L\) & Read \\
    \midrule
    Cruise & 0.423M \(\rightarrow\) 2.159M \(\rightarrow\) 2.773M \(\rightarrow\) 8.505M & 2.02\% & 0.57\% & Mixed \\
    Takeoff & 0.423M \(\rightarrow\) 2.159M \(\rightarrow\) 2.773M \(\rightarrow\) 33.901M & 3.13\% & 0.58\% & {\color{warnred}Sensitive} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item Former ``fine'' meshes are relabeled Medium 2 because they sat very close to the medium meshes in total cell count; former ``extra-fine'' meshes are reported as Fine.
    \item The reported \(\Delta C_D\) and \(\Delta C_L\) values are the final Medium 2 \(\rightarrow\) Fine changes, so this section is presented as mesh sensitivity rather than formal grid independence.
    \item Retained wall-layer cells remained zero, so the study captures coefficient sensitivity of the present setup rather than full boundary-layer independence.
  \end{itemize}

  \sectionbar{Claim Boundary and Next Steps}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The main result is an integrated, traceable preliminary-design environment for flying-wing aircraft rather than a stand-alone new solver.
    \item The validation evidence supports a narrow claim: the present low-order workflow preserves lift and trim trends better than absolute drag for thick blended-centerbody configurations.
    \item The dominant missing term is centerbody-dominated pressure and interference drag rather than outer-wing skin friction alone.
    \item Next work should focus on stronger centerbody drag modeling, retained wall-layer verification, and comparison against additional published or experimental cases.
  \end{itemize}
